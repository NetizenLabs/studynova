# The Ebbinghaus Forgetting Curve

Have you ever spent a whole weekend memorizing 100 flashcards, aced your self-test on Sunday night, only to sit down for your exam on Wednesday and completely blank? 

You didn't fail because you're bad at studying. You failed because of **The Forgetting Curve**.

In 1885, a German psychologist named Hermann Ebbinghaus discovered a depressing fact about the human brain: **we forget 70% of what we learn within 24 hours**. Unless you actively review the information, your brain assumes it's unimportant and deletes it to save space.

So, how do you hack the forgetting curve? **Spaced Repetition.**

---

## What is Spaced Repetition?

Spaced repetition is an evidence-based learning technique where you review information at systematically increasing intervals. 

Instead of cramming the same flashcard 10 times in one day (which only stores the information in your short-term memory), you review it:
- 1 day later
- 3 days later
- 1 week later
- 2 weeks later
- 1 month later

Every time you review the information right when you are on the verge of forgetting it, the neural pathway in your brain becomes stronger. Eventually, the memory becomes permanent.

---

## Why Rereading Notes is a Trap

Most students study by rereading their textbook highlights or their lecture notes over and over again. This creates a dangerous psychological phenomenon called **The Illusion of Fluency**. 

When you read a highlighted sentence, your brain says, *"Oh yeah, I recognize that!"* You mistake **recognition** for **recall**. 

Recognition is easy; the answer is literally right in front of you. Recall is hard; you have to pull the answer out of thin air. Exams test recall, not recognition. Spaced repetition forces you to practice recall.

---

## How to Build a Spaced Repetition System

You don't need to do complex math to figure out when to review your notes. You just need a system.

### Option 1: The Analog System (The Leitner System)
If you prefer physical flashcards, you can use the Leitner box system. 
1. Get three physical boxes (Box 1, Box 2, Box 3).
2. Put all new flashcards in Box 1. You review Box 1 **every day**.
3. If you get a card right in Box 1, move it to Box 2. You review Box 2 **every 3 days**.
4. If you get a card right in Box 2, move it to Box 3. You review Box 3 **once a week**.
5. *Crucial rule:* If you ever get a card wrong, it goes all the way back to Box 1!

### Option 2: The Digital System (Anki / Quizlet)
The easiest way to use spaced repetition is to let an algorithm do the math for you. Apps like **Anki** use spaced repetition algorithms to automatically schedule your flashcards. 

All you do is open the app every day, and it feeds you exactly the cards you are about to forget.

## Combining Spaced Repetition with Active Recall

Spaced repetition tells you *when* to study. Active recall tells you *how* to study. 

Instead of just looking at a flashcard, you must actively force your brain to retrieve the answer before you flip it over. The mental strain you feel when you're trying to remember the answer? That strain is literally the feeling of your brain building new neural connections.
